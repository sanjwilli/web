Caribbean Identity

“The Caribbean today is characterized by grave intolerance and mistrust at all levels. Exploring our history, society and concepts of identity, give your informed perspective on this statement.”

From pre 1492 also known as pre-history until today the existence of people and their way of life in the Caribbean region is constantly being revised by historians. Arising from this today is the issue as to the existence of inter-relations between the Caribbean people? If so, how is it characterized? Is there trust and tolerance versus grave mistrust and intolerance? Research reveals that like the peoples of the pre- colonialization and colonialization eras, their existence was strongly driven by the need to survive and clinging to their own kind. Relatively today like the colonial period, the Caribbean society is driven by rules and morals; the latter which is unique to whichever country or people whilst the former is based on conformance to natural laws that was received from the English, Dutch, Spanish and Portuguese Empires. Some countries within the region having gone through more than one colonization have developed a hybrid system. Essentially, this sums up the complexities of the Caribbean.

The Caribbean is one of the most diverse regions, the “melting pot” a combination of varying cultures, people and experiences similarly mirroring definitions vantages. The most popular comes from geographers but more suitable is the historical definition.

“… the Caribbean… based on the concept of the ‘Caribbean Basin’, where the central identifying feature is the Caribbean Sea rimmed by mainland territories of Central and South America… stretching from 60 degrees W to 90 degrees of the Greenwich Meridian… from 5 degrees N of the Equator to beyond 25 degrees N.” “Caribbean diaspora: The word ‘diaspora’ is Greek in origin and means ‘to scatter’. In Caribbean studies, the Diasporic Caribbean refers to a group of people who reside in another geographical location, for instance, people of the Caribbean ancestry who reside in European metropolitan countries – the USA, Canada, among others- who share an emotional connection to their homeland” “…three main political systems: 1. Associated states – These countries are not independent but enjoy all rights and privileges of the countries that govern them; for example, Anguilla. 3. Language embodies these cultural differences and presents the major distinguishing factor that delimits the Caribbean from Latin America.”
Shortcomings
“..the coordinates of longitude and latitude are interpreted subjectively. …Belize is not in the Caribbean Basin but is a large land mass in Central America and is a part of the Caribbean” The dilemma between fact and sense of identity. Awareness of identity, where one sees themselves relative to others. This view does not consider the majority of society and the common way of life and is particularly focused on the governance. “In defining the Caribbean using the historical criteria, the European influence looms larger than that of the indigenous peoples, even though they were brought here later as indentured labourers.” Also the introduction of the diasporic definition with the rise in migration and Caribbean communities outside of the region.

Now that we have defined the region in order to get a deeper understanding of the Caribbean society we question what is a society and more importantly how does it function? Sociologists have posited that a society is a group of people whose members interact, reside in a definable area, and share a culture. The revisionists share this view by further establishing that the civilization was existent in the Caribbean from primitive days outlining structured and identifiable subset cultures and functional societies so that culture encompassed groups sharing practices, values, beliefs, norms and artifacts. More specifically the sociologists painted societies based on varying views. The Functionalist view is that social institutions such as the family, school, religion, work and political are the drivers of a functional society. The Marxist perspective rests on the division of a people based on economic and social classes and coined the bourgeois concept. The Feminist views society as unequal based on the role of females vis-a-vis men in the functioning of society. The combination of these views show that there is an interrelationship amongst people particularly the Caribbean. This therefore requires trust and tolerance because of the diversity in language, locations, cultures and independent pursuits.

The Caribbean Identity – Trust and Tolerance, an historical perspective

In the Caribbean, the concepts of country, nation and State is the genesis of the need of belonging to a region. The difficulty is in defining a Caribbean identity as one unified people that share a common historical background and region. A part from the Caribbean people defining their identity based on a designated geographical area, do they truly share ascribed cultural identities. One postulates that each country has its own ethno cultural group’s differentiation. Thus, identity formation and its sustenance as time passes is relational and conflicting. People in general tend to cling to their own and this promotes the “we-they” concepts. Thus within each Caribbean State, there is an existence of subcultural conflicts in identity intensifying the oppositional oneness as a region. Research as shown that the Caribbean people are easy to identify with their own outside of the region than they would normally do in their homeland thus creating the fact of identity and double diasporic experience. In backtracking we note that the indigenous peoples habituated the Caribbean and formed communities largely driven by ethnic commonalities. Each had their own means of survival. The Neolithics were gatherers and producers whilst the Palaeolithics were nomads until they settled because their groups became too large. The latter survived from hunting and fishing. Soon the Saladoid peoples (Tainos and Kalinagos) emerged. The Tainos, a political and socially organized group spoke Arawakan and were mostly located in the Greater Antilles specializing in tools, axe and bladed and traded with other cultural groups. Conversely, the Kalinagos were more of expansionist and aggressively pursued this phase by moving up the Caribbean. Here we see an existence of different cultures, each having their own languages, way of life, practices, lifestyles and views. Today, we see remnants of these people and a continuum of varying cultures. The topical issue was there grave mistrust and intolerance in the Caribbean at that time. Historians never really painted this but is was thwarted by myths that fostered mistrust and intolerance and such a picture is what resonated in the combined efforts of the peoples to either welcome or fight against the Europeans arrival into the Caribbean in the early 15 th century. Trust existed merely in the reliance on closely networked communities where there were defined roles of members of families and chiefs. Bartering existed and soon trading was a sustainable means of gathering goods and services for which they never specialized in. One fact that eroded the trust and tolerance between the peoples was the influx of the European ethnocentric and androcentric views. Eurocentrism was merely treating the Caribbean indigenous peoples as inferior based on own culture and lifestyle comparison such lack of covering of the body was the dress code at the time versus fully clothed Europeans. Androcentrism was grounded on the males’ view of the females when they arrived as being sexual because they were not fully clothed resulting in sexual advances and violations of the women’s rights. The culture of the Indigenous Caribbean was slowly diluted with these views and new forced cultures emerged.
